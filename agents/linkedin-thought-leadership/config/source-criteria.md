# Source Credibility Criteria

## PURPOSE
This document defines what makes a source credible for use in LinkedIn thought leadership articles.

---

## THREE-TIER CREDIBILITY SYSTEM

### TIER 1: Highly Credible (Preferred)

**Characteristics:**
- Independent research and analysis
- Peer-reviewed or professionally vetted
- Transparent methodology
- Authoritative in the field
- No commercial bias

**Source Types:**

**Academic & Research:**
- Peer-reviewed journal articles
- University research papers
- Government research labs
- Independent research institutes

**Standards Bodies:**
- ISO standards documentation
- Industry standard specifications
- Professional association guidelines

**Professional Organizations:**
- Institute of Asset Management (IAM)
- Global Forum on Maintenance and Asset Management (GFMAM)
- Asset Management Council (Australia)
- URISA (Urban and Regional Information Systems Association)
- GITA (Geospatial Information & Technology Association)

**Government Agencies:**
- Infrastructure departments (DOT, utilities commissions)
- National mapping agencies
- Government audit offices
- Public infrastructure authorities

**Independent Research Firms:**
- Deloitte (industry studies, not consulting marketing)
- McKinsey (research reports)
- Gartner (analyst reports)
- Forrester (research)
- Academic-affiliated research groups

**Examples:**
- "Digital Twins for Infrastructure Sustainability" - Institute for Sustainable Infrastructure (2025)
- ISO 55000:2024 Asset Management Standard
- "GIS Maturity Assessment Study" - URISA Journal
- Deloitte Infrastructure Study 2024

**How to Cite:**
```
According to research from the Institute of Asset Management, organizations implementing digital twins report a 15% reduction in maintenance costs.
```

---

### TIER 2: Credible with Context (Use Judiciously)

**Characteristics:**
- Reputable but potentially biased
- Commercial interests but valuable data
- Industry news from established publications
- Vendor research if methodology is sound

**Source Types:**

**Established Technology Vendors:**
- Esri (GIS industry leader)
- Bentley Systems (infrastructure software)
- IBM (asset management platforms)
- Autodesk (BIM/CAD)
**Note:** Use for technical capabilities and case studies, but validate benefits claims independently

**Industry Publications:**
- GIS Professional Magazine
- Infrastructure Magazine
- Asset Management Insights
- Engineering News-Record
**Note:** Good for trends and case studies, less authoritative for ROI claims

**Conference Proceedings:**
- IAM Annual Conference papers
- Esri User Conference presentations
- URISA Conference proceedings
**Note:** Peer-reviewed conference papers stronger than vendor presentations

**Professional White Papers:**
- Technical white papers with methodology
- Industry reports from credible consultancies
- Case studies from named organizations
**Note:** Check author credentials and methodology

**Trade Organizations:**
- ASCE (American Society of Civil Engineers)
- ICE (Institution of Civil Engineers)
- Engineers Australia

**Examples:**
- Esri case study: "City of Portland 3D Asset Management Implementation"
- "State of GIS 2025" - Geospatial World
- IBM Maximo white paper: "Digital Twins in Utilities"

**How to Cite:**
```
Northumbrian Water, a UK utility provider, reduced site visits by 40% using 3D asset models integrated with their CMMS platform (Matterport case study, 2025).

[Note: Acknowledge it's a vendor case study but real organization with real results]
```

**Red Flags to Watch:**
- Vendor-only sources with no independent validation
- Marketing materials disguised as research
- Unnamed "clients" or anonymous case studies
- Methodology not disclosed
- Suspiciously perfect results

---

### TIER 3: Use Sparingly or Avoid

**Characteristics:**
- Minimal vetting or peer review
- Clear commercial bias
- Questionable methodology or none stated
- Unverifiable claims

**Source Types to Use Sparingly:**

**Vendor Marketing Materials:**
- Product brochures
- Sales presentations
- Marketing blog posts
**Use only if:** Describing product features (not benefits), real customer names provided

**General Blogs & Opinion Pieces:**
- Medium articles (unless from verified expert)
- LinkedIn posts
- Personal blogs
**Use only if:** Author has strong credentials, claims can be verified elsewhere

**Social Media:**
- Twitter/X posts
- LinkedIn updates
- YouTube videos
**Use only if:** From verified industry leaders, pointing to trends (not data)

**Source Types to Avoid:**

**Never Use:**
- Content farms or AI-generated content sites
- Anonymous sources
- Broken or inaccessible links
- Outdated information (pre-2020) presented as current
- Sources that can't be verified
- Contradicted by credible sources

## VALIDATION CHECKLIST

### Before Using Any Source, Ask:

**Authority:**
- [ ] Who is the author/organization?
- [ ] What are their credentials?
- [ ] Are they recognized experts in this field?
- [ ] Any conflicts of interest?

**Accuracy:**
- [ ] Are claims supported with data?
- [ ] Is methodology explained?
- [ ] Can data be verified elsewhere?
- [ ] Do other sources confirm this?

**Currency:**
- [ ] When was this published?
- [ ] Is information still current?
- [ ] For trends: Is this 2024-2025 data?
- [ ] For case studies: Implementation timeframe?

**Purpose:**
- [ ] Why was this created?
- [ ] Is it research, marketing, or opinion?
- [ ] Who funded this research?
- [ ] Is there commercial bias?

**Coverage:**
- [ ] Is scope appropriate for claim?
- [ ] Sample size adequate?
- [ ] Geographic relevance (Australia vs. global)?
- [ ] Sector relevance (infrastructure vs. general)?

## SOURCE SELECTION STRATEGY

### Ideal Source Mix for Article:

**Minimum Requirements:**
- 3-5 sources total
- At least 2 Tier 1 sources
- At least 1 with quantified ROI data
- At least 1 case study with named organization

**Optimal Mix:**
- 2 Tier 1 sources (standards, research, govt)
- 2 Tier 2 sources (vendor case studies, industry news)
- 1 Tier 2-3 source if needed for specific detail

**Example Optimal Mix:**
1. **Tier 1:** Deloitte study on digital twins ROI
2. **Tier 1:** ISO 55000 standard documentation
3. **Tier 2:** Northumbrian Water case study (Matterport)
4. **Tier 2:** Esri white paper on 3D GIS capabilities
5. **Tier 2:** Industry news article on market trends

## REGIONAL SOURCE PRIORITIES

### Australia/Pacific Context:
**Prioritize:**
- Asset Management Council (Australia)
- Engineers Australia
- Australian infrastructure agencies
- Regional case studies (ports, utilities, councils)

**Why:** More relatable to Pilbara Ports context and Australian GIS professionals

### Global Context Acceptable When:
- Australian-specific data unavailable
- International standards (ISO)
- Major technology trends
- Clear applicability to Australia

## SPECIAL CASES

### Market Sizing Reports:
**Accept from:** Gartner, Forrester, Deloitte, McKinsey, academic studies
**Be cautious of:** Marketing research firms, vendor-commissioned studies
**Note:** Different reports often have different numbersâ€”cite the most credible, note range if large variance

### Technology Vendor Data:
**Accept:** Technical capability descriptions, product features
**Verify independently:** ROI claims, cost savings, efficiency gains
**Prefer:** Customer case studies over vendor claims

### Conference Presentations:
**Accept:** Peer-reviewed conference papers
**Use cautiously:** Vendor presentations (good for trends, not definitive data)
**Prefer:** Published proceedings over slide decks

## DOCUMENTING SOURCES

### For Each Source Used:

**Required Information:**
- Full title
- Author/organization
- Publication date (at least year)
- URL (must be accessible)
- Key data point extracted

**Format:**
```
1. Digital Twins Drive Cost Savings in Infrastructure - Deloitte (2024)
   URL: https://www2.deloitte.com/us/en/insights/...
   Key data: 15% reduction in maintenance costs, improved operational efficiency
   Tier: 1 (Highly Credible)
```

## SOURCE REFRESH POLICY

### When to Update Sources:

**Annually:** 
- Market sizing and trend data
- Technology adoption statistics
- Maturity model updates

**As Needed:**
- Case studies (keep most recent 2-3 years)
- ROI data (verify still accurate)
- Standards documentation (check for revisions)

**When Source Gets Old:**
- Replace pre-2020 data unless historical context
- Flag if source >3 years old for trends
- Standards: Use most recent version
